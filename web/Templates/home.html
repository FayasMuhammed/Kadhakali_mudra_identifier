About The Website

Leveraging the power of computer vision, this website brings a modern approach to Kathakali, the classical Indian dance form celebrated for its expressive hand gestures, or mudras. By combining MediaPipe for hand tracking and OpenCV for real-time image processing, the system captures live video from a camera, identifying key hand landmarks to accurately recognize and interpret each unique mudra. These gestures, each with distinct configurations of fingers and hand positions, are mapped and labeled instantly, allowing for precise, real-time/ mudra recognition. This fusion of traditional art and advanced technology creates an interactive and educational platform that supports performers, students, and audiences in exploring the intricacies of Kathakali. Beyond preservation, it makes the art form accessible to new learners around the world, offering real-time feedback and insights that enrich the appreciation of this cultural heritage. The application fosters a deeper connection with Kathakali by transforming learning and practice into an engaging, tech-driven experience.

What is MediaPipe and OpenCV

MediaPipe and OpenCV work together to recognize and track Kathakali hand gestures, or mudras, in real-time. MediaPipe identifies key points on the hands from the camera feed, allowing the system to detect and interpret each unique mudra. Meanwhile, OpenCV processes the video input, manages frame-by-frame analysis, and displays real-time feedback on the screen, showing users the recognized mudras as they perform them. This combination enables accurate, responsive mudra recognition through seamless integration of hand tracking and video processing.

About Kathakali

Kathakali (IAST: Kathakaḷi Malayalam: കഥകളി pronunciationⓘ) is a traditional form of classical Indian dance, and one of the most complex forms of Indian theatre. It is a play of verses . These Verses called Kathakali literature or Attakatha . Mostly played in the courts of Kings and Temple festivals . Hence it known as suvarna art forms . This performance uses the navarasas of Bharathan, the Guru of Indian theatre . Makeup and costumes are very unique and huge . Normally it represent Kerala Art . It is native to the Malayalam-language southwestern region of Kerala and is almost entirely practiced by Malayali people.

The fully developed style of Kathakali originated around the 16th century, but its roots are in the temple and folk arts (such as Krishnanattam and religious drama of the kingdom of the Zamorin of Calicut) of the southwestern Indian peninsula, which are traceable to at least the 1st millennium CE.A Kathakali performance, like all classical dance arts of India, synthesizes music, vocal performers, choreography and hand and facial gestures together to express ideas. However, Kathakali differs in that it also incorporates movements from ancient Indian martial arts and athletic traditions of South India. Kathakali also differs in that the structure and details of its art form developed in the courts and theatres of Hindu principalities, unlike other classical Indian dances which primarily developed in Hindu temples and monastic schools. The traditional themes of the Kathakali are folk stories, religious legends and spiritual ideas from the Hindu epics and the Puranas. The vocal performance has traditionally been performed in Sanskritised Malayalam. In modern compositions, Indian Kathakali have included women artistes, and adapted Western stories and plays such as those by Shakespeare. In 2011, a performance expressing Christian doctrine was staged for the first time in Kerala.
